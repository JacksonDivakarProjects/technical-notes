### **Priority Question 5 (Unit 5 - Emerging Ethical Challenges)**

**Q: Discuss the ethical dilemmas in modern scientific advancements, specifically focusing on Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Environmental Sustainability. How can scientists address these challenges?**

---

### **13-Mark Answer Structure**

**1. Introduction**
* **Context:** Science and technology are double-edged swords. While they drive progress, they also introduce complex ethical risks that did not exist 50 years ago.
* **The Dilemma:** The pace of innovation often outstrips the pace of regulation. Scientists effectively "play God" by creating intelligent machines or altering the planet's climate, making ethical oversight crucial.

**2. Ethical Implications of Artificial Intelligence (AI)**
* *As a Data Science student, you can write this section very well.*
* **Algorithmic Bias:** AI systems learn from historic data. If that data is racist or sexist, the AI will be too (e.g., facial recognition failing on dark-skinned faces).
* **Privacy & Surveillance:** The ethical line between "security" and "mass surveillance" is blurring. Scientists must protect user anonymity.
* **Accountability (The "Black Box" Problem):** If a self-driving car crashes, who is responsible? The programmer? The car manufacturer? Or the AI?
* **Job Displacement:** The ethical responsibility to consider the human cost of automation and advocate for re-skilling.

**3. Ethical Implications of Environmental Sustainability**
* **Intergenerational Equity:** We have a moral duty to leave the planet in a habitable condition for future generations. Ruining the environment for short-term gain is an ethical violation.
* **The Precautionary Principle:** When the health of the environment is at stake (e.g., releasing a new chemical), lack of full scientific certainty shall not be used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures to prevent degradation.
* **Resource Depletion:** Scientists developing new products must consider the "Lifecycle" of the product—where do the raw materials come from, and will it end up in a landfill?

**4. Diagram Recommendation**
* *Is a diagram needed?* **Yes.** A "Balance Scale" works perfectly here.
* **Draw this:** A weighing scale. On one side, put **"Innovation/Profit"**. On the other side, put **"Ethics/Sustainability"**. The pointer should be in the middle, labeled **"Responsible Science"**.


**5. Solutions: How Scientists Can Address This**
* **Ethics by Design:** Integrating ethical checks *during* the development phase, not after. (e.g., testing AI for bias before release).
* **Green Chemistry:** Designing chemical products and processes that reduce or eliminate the use of hazardous substances.
* **Public Engagement:** Engaging with policymakers to create laws that regulate these new technologies (e.g., AI Regulations, Carbon Taxes).

**6. Conclusion**
Technological feasibility does not mean ethical permissibility. Just because we *can* build something (like a clone or a fully autonomous weapon) doesn't mean we *should*. The role of the modern scientist is to be a "Guardian of the Future," ensuring that advancements in AI and industry serve humanity without destroying the planet or human dignity.
