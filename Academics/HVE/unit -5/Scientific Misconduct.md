### **Priority Question 4 (Unit 5 - Scientific Ethics)**

**Q: Define Scientific Misconduct and analyze its various forms (FFP). How does "Bias" compromise research integrity, and what are the consequences of such unethical behavior?**

---

### **13-Mark Answer Structure**

**1. Introduction**
* **Definition:** Scientific Misconduct represents the violation of the standard codes of scholarly conduct and ethical behavior in professional scientific research.
* **Context:** Science relies on **Trust**. If data is manipulated, the entire foundation of knowledge crumbles. Misconduct is not just "honest error" or "difference of opinion"—it is the *deliberate* intent to deceive.

**2. The "Trilogy" of Scientific Misconduct (FFP)**
*This is the standard international classification. You must remember the acronym **FFP**.*

* **1. Fabrication (Making it up):**
    * The act of making up data or results and recording or reporting them.
    * *Example:* A researcher claims they did 100 experiments but actually did zero, just inventing the numbers in a spreadsheet.
* **2. Falsification (Manipulating it):**
    * Manipulating research materials, equipment, or processes, or changing/omitting data so the research is not accurately represented.
    * *Example:* A data scientist deleting "outlier" data points just to make the graph look perfect or a smooth curve.
* **3. Plagiarism (Taking credit):**
    * The appropriation of another person's ideas, processes, results, or words without giving appropriate credit.
    * *Self-Plagiarism:* Re-using your own previously published work as if it were new.

**3. The Role of Bias in Research**
*Bias is often subtle but equally dangerous.*
* **Confirmation Bias:** The tendency to search for, interpret, or recall information in a way that confirms one's pre-existing beliefs or hypotheses. (e.g., ignoring data that disproves your theory).
* **Selection Bias:** Choosing a specific subset of data that supports your conclusion while ignoring the rest.
* **Outcome:** Bias leads to **False Positives**—findings that look true but are actually statistical flukes.

**4. Diagram Recommendation**
* *Is a diagram needed?* **Yes.** Visualizing the "FFP" model helps the examiner quickly identify you know the core concept.
* **Draw this:** A triangle labeled **"Scientific Misconduct"** in the center. The three corners are **Fabrication**, **Falsification**, and **Plagiarism**. Under each, write 2-3 keywords (e.g., under Fabrication: "Creating Fake Data").



**5. Consequences of Misconduct**
* **For the Scientist:** Loss of credibility, termination of employment, revocation of degrees, and legal action.
* **For Society:**
    * **Wasted Resources:** Millions of dollars/rupees are spent trying to replicate fake studies.
    * **Public Harm:** If medical or safety research is faked (e.g., a faked drug trial), it can directly lead to loss of human life.
    * **Erosion of Trust:** The public loses faith in scientific institutions (e.g., anti-vaccine movements often stem from distrust caused by past unethical studies).

**6. Conclusion**
Research integrity is the currency of science. While pressure to "publish or perish" often pushes researchers toward shortcuts, adhering to ethical standards—avoiding FFP and mitigating bias—is non-negotiable. True science requires the courage to report the truth, even when the data contradicts our hopes.
