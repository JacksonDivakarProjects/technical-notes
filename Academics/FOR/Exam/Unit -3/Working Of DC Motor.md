A DC motor's construction involves a stationary part (stator) and a rotating part (rotor/armature), with key components like the yoke, field poles, field windings, armature core, armature windings, commutator, and brushes; the interaction between the magnetic field from the stator and current in the armature windings creates torque, while the commutator and brushes ensure continuous rotation by reversing current direction. 

  

Stator (Stationary Part)

- Yoke/Frame: The outer casing, providing mechanical support and a path for magnetic flux; made of cast iron or steel.
- Field Poles: Protrusions (pole cores with pole shoes) attached to the yoke, creating the main magnetic field.
- Field Windings: Copper coils wrapped around the pole cores; energized by DC to produce the magnetic field (or permanent magnets are used). 

Rotor (Rotating Part)

- Armature Core: A cylindrical core (often laminated silicon steel) with slots to hold windings, mounted on the shaft.
- Armature Windings: Insulated conductors in the armature slots where voltage is induced and current flows, generating torque.
- **Commutator:** A cylindrical structure made of hard-drawn copper segments insulated from each other by mica. It acts as a mechanical rectifier, reversing the direction of current in the armature windings periodically to ensure a continuous, unidirectional torque.
- **Brushes:** Carbon or graphite contacts that maintain a sliding electrical connection between the stationary external circuit and the rotating commutator, allowing current to be supplied to the armature coils.




Other Components

- Shaft: Supports the armature and transmits mechanical power.
- Bearings: Allow the shaft and armature to rotate smoothly. 

Working Principle

1. DC current flows through the field windings (or permanent magnets create the field).
2. Current is fed to the armature windings via the brushes and commutator.
3. The magnetic field interacts with the current-carrying armature conductors, producing force (Lorentz Force).
4. This force creates torque, causing the armature to rotate.
5. The commutator reverses current in the coils as they pass the poles, maintaining continuous rotation in one direction.
