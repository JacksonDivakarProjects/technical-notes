1. **Explain the Total Quality Management (TQM) Framework and its fundamental components.**
    
    - **Explanation:** TQM is an approach to improving the competitiveness, effectiveness, and flexibility of an entire organisation. It involves planning, organising, and understanding each activity, depending on individual contribution at every level. Key to TQM are understanding and commitment by senior management, effective leadership, teamwork, sound process management, and appropriate improvement methods and tools. The basic concepts include a **committed and involved management**, an **unwavering focus on the customer** (internal and external), **effective involvement and utilisation of the entire workforce**, **continuous improvement** of business and production processes, **treating suppliers as partners**, and **establishing performance measures** for processes. The "Oakland TQM Model" integrates "four Ps" – **Planning**, **Performance**, **People**, and **Process** – with "three Cs" – **Culture**, **Communication**, and **Commitment** – to guide organisations towards excellence.

2. **Discuss the contributions of major Quality Gurus (Deming, Juran, Taguchi) to Total Quality Management.**
    
    - **Explanation:** Several quality experts, or "gurus," laid the foundation for TQM.
        - **W. Edwards Deming:** He is considered the best-known quality expert globally and is credited with providing the foundation for Japan's quality miracle and economic resurgence. His **14 points** offer a theory for management to improve quality, productivity, and competitive position.
        - **Joseph M. Juran:** He is known for the **Juran Trilogy**, which primarily aims to achieve quality improvement through three key components: **planning**, **control**, and **continuous improvement**. It emphasizes embedding quality into processes to meet customer needs.
        - **Genichi Taguchi:** He developed the **loss function concept**, which integrates cost, target, and variation into a single metric. He also introduced the **signal-to-noise ratio** as a proactive counterpart to the loss function. His philosophy's cornerstone is the robust design of parameters and tolerances.
3. **Elaborate on the Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle and its application in continuous improvement.**
    
    - **Explanation:** The PDSA cycle, originally developed by Shewhart and modified by Deming, is a powerful technique for continuous improvement. It is also known as the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle, with "Study" replacing "Check" to emphasize analyzing results.
        - **Plan:** Carefully determine what needs to be done. This phase involves identifying the improvement opportunity, forming a dedicated team, and clearly defining the scope of the problem.
        - **Do:** Implement the planned changes. This involves carrying out the solutions and collecting data on the new process.
        - **Study:** Analyze the results to determine if the plan worked as intended and if the outcomes were different from expectations.
        - **Act:** Based on the study's findings, take action by standardising successful solutions and planning for future improvements. This leads to developing an improved plan and repeating the cycle.
    - **Application:** The PDSA cycle is used in various contexts, including Nissan's TQM objectives management process, to drive continuous improvement in organisations by systematically addressing problems.

5. **Describe the DMAIC methodology within Six Sigma and its significance.**
    
    - **Explanation:** DMAIC is a structured, five-phase framework used in Six Sigma for process improvement.
        - **Define:** Project scope and goals are articulated, often in terms of desired outcomes.
        - **Measure:** Current process performance is mapped and measured to understand value addition and waste.
        - **Analyze:** The gap between current and desired performance is analysed, identifying problems and prioritising opportunities.
        - **Improve:** Improvement solutions are generated and validated to realise opportunities and fix root causes permanently.
        - **Control:** The improved processes are implemented to sustain performance improvement, learning lessons, and establishing continuous reassessment.
    - **Significance:** DMAIC is crucial because it provides a disciplined, fact-based approach to problem-solving and improvement. It helps deliver breakthrough benefits in the short term, distinguishing Six Sigma from traditional TQM, by bringing a structured approach and demanding management commitment to achieve real performance and bottom-line improvements.

6. **Outline the Benchmarking process, including its stages and benefits for an organization.**
    
    - **Explanation:** Benchmarking is a process that measures an organisation's products, services, and processes to establish targets, priorities, and improvements, ultimately leading to competitive advantage or cost reductions.
    - **Process Stages:** The benchmarking process typically involves five main stages:
        1. **Plan the study:** Select the processes for benchmarking, form a team, define benchmarks and measures, identify best competitors, and document current processes.
        2. **Collect data and information:** Decide on data collection methodology (e.g., desk research, site visits), record current performance, and identify partners.
        3. **Analyse data:** Identify best practices and the enablers that deliver outstanding performance, capturing learning and key outcomes.
        4. **Adapt findings:** Assist participants in adapting conclusions to their own organisation.
        5. **Review performance and the study:** Monitor results, assess outcomes, review benchmarks, share experiences, and identify further improvement opportunities.
    - **Benefits:** It helps to systematically identify the "vital few" areas for improvement by comparing against "best-in-class" organisations. It makes the case for change objectively and allows for quantitative prediction of change effects.

7. **Differentiate between Design FMEA and Process FMEA, and explain the steps involved in an FMEA procedure including the Risk Priority Number (RPN) calculation.**
    
    - **Explanation:** Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (FMEA) is a proactive analytical team tool used for reliability improvement by identifying foreseeable failure modes and planning for their elimination.
        - **Design FMEA (DFMEA):** This type of FMEA aids in the design process. It identifies potential failures in product design to ensure reliability before production begins. It helps rank failures based on their impact on the product, uncovering oversights, misjudgments, and errors in the design phase.
        - **Process FMEA (PFMEA):** This focuses on potential failures in manufacturing or operational processes. Its purpose is to assure that potential failure modes and their associated causes have been considered and addressed within the production or service delivery process to improve efficiency.
    - **FMEA Procedure Steps:**
        1. **Identify Item/Function:** Clearly define the product, process, or system being analysed.
        2. **Identify Potential Failure Mode:** List how the item might fail to meet design criteria or cause failure in a higher-level system.
        3. **Identify Potential Effect(s) of Failure:** Determine the consequences of each failure mode.
        4. **Assign Severity (S):** Rate the seriousness of the failure's effect (1=low, 10=high). This rating applies only to the effect, not the failure mode itself.
        5. **Identify Potential Cause(s)/Mechanism(s) of Failure:** List all possible reasons why the failure might occur.
        6. **Assign Occurrence (O):** Rate the likelihood of the failure cause happening (1=low, 10=high).
        7. **Identify Current Controls:** List existing methods for detecting or preventing the failure.
        8. **Assign Detection (D):** Rate the likelihood that the failure will be detected before reaching the customer (1=easy to detect, 10=very difficult to detect).
        9. **Calculate Risk Priority Number (RPN):** **RPN = Severity (S) × Occurrence (O) × Detection (D)**. This value helps rank order concerns for corrective action, ranging from 1 to 1000.
        10. **Recommend Actions:** Propose corrective or preventive actions to reduce S, O, or D, prioritising high severity first, then high occurrence, and then high detection.

9. **Discuss the importance of Employee Involvement and Empowerment in TQM, including recognition and reward systems.**
    
    - **Explanation:** Employee involvement is a core concept of TQM, emphasizing the effective utilisation of the entire workforce. It is essential for the success of TQM efforts.
        - **Empowerment:** This creates an environment where individuals have the ability, confidence, and commitment to take responsibility and ownership for improving processes and satisfying customer requirements within defined boundaries. Leaders play a crucial role by delegating authority and encouraging employees to take ownership.
        - **Recognition and Reward:** These are vital for employee motivation. **Recognition** involves publicly acknowledging positive contributions, using verbal and written praise or symbolic items. **Reward** includes tangible items (e.g., tickets, cash awards) to promote desirable behaviour. Together, they form a system that communicates to employees that they are valued members of the organisation, and leaders should recognise teams achieving quality goals.
    - **Importance:** Such involvement fosters a sense of ownership, improves communication, builds trust, and develops interdependence within the organisation, ultimately leading to greater success in TQM implementation.
10. **Describe the 5S methodology for workplace organization and the principles of Kaizen for continuous improvement.**
    
    - **Explanation:**
        - **5S Methodology:** Originating in Japan, 5S is a system for workplace organisation and standardisation. It consists of five practices:
            - **Seiri (Sort):** Removing unnecessary tools, materials, and documents from work areas, keeping only essential items.
            - **Seiton (Set in Order):** Arranging essential items for easy access and efficient use (ergonomics).
            - **Seiso (Shine):** Thoroughly cleaning the workspace and establishing standards for maintaining cleanliness.
            - **Seiketsu (Standardise):** Establishing consistent practices and procedures for the first three S's to ensure they are maintained.
            - **Shitsuke (Sustain):** Maintaining discipline to adhere to the established standards and continuously improve them.
            - **Application:** In the UK health sector, 5S has been adapted to a 'CANDO' approach (Clean up, Arranging, Neatness, Discipline, Ongoing improvement).
        - **Kaizen Principles:** Kaizen emphasizes continuous improvement, often through small, incremental changes involving all employees. Key principles include:
            - **Being open-minded** and maintaining a **positive attitude**.
            - **Rejecting excuses** and actively seeking solutions.
            - **Asking "Why? Why? Why? Why? and Why again?"** to identify root causes, emphasizing that there are no stupid questions.
            - **Taking action** and implementing solutions immediately, rather than striving for perfection initially.
            - **Utilising all the team's knowledge** and **disregarding rank**, treating everyone as equal contributors.
