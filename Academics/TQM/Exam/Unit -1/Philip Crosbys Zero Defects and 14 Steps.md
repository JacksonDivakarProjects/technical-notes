### **Question 4: Philip Crosby’s "Zero Defects" & 14 Steps**

**Importance:**

> _"Philip Crosby completes the 'Holy Trinity' of Quality Gurus (Deming, Juran, Crosby). His approach is the most commercially popular because it is simple: 'Quality is Free' and the goal is 'Zero Defects'. This 13-mark question requires you to explain his 'Four Absolutes' and then list his '14 Steps'. It is very high-scoring because it is structured."_

#### **The Answer**

1. Introduction

Philip Crosby was a legendary quality consultant who wrote the famous book "Quality is Free". He argued that quality does not cost money; it is the lack of quality (rework, scrap, warranty claims) that costs money. His philosophy focuses on prevention rather than inspection.

2. The Four Absolutes of Quality

Before writing the steps, you must define his core philosophy:

1. **Definition of Quality:** Quality is defined as **Conformance to Requirements**, not "goodness" or "elegance."
    
2. **System of Quality:** The system for causing quality is **Prevention**, not appraisal (inspection).
    
3. **Performance Standard:** The only performance standard is **Zero Defects** (ZD). "Close enough" is not acceptable.
    
4. **Measurement:** The measurement of quality is the **Price of Non-Conformance (PONC)** (i.e., how much money we lose by doing things wrong).
    

5. Crosby’s 14 Steps for Quality Improvement

To implement the Absolutes, Crosby prescribed a 14-step program.

- **Step 1: Management Commitment:**
    
    - Top management must be convinced of the need for quality and must communicate this commitment to all employees. It cannot just be a "delegated" task.
        
- **Step 2: Quality Improvement Team (QIT):**
    
    - Form a team composed of department heads to oversee improvements. They act as the "engine" of the quality drive.
        
- **Step 3: Quality Measurement:**
    
    - Establish measurements for every activity (Manufacturing, Sales, Admin) to determine where current and potential problems lie. You cannot improve what you cannot measure.
        
- **Step 4: Cost of Quality Evaluation:**
    
    - Calculate the "Price of Non-Conformance" (PONC). This shows management how much money is being flushed down the drain due to errors, creating the financial justification for TQM.
        
- **Step 5: Quality Awareness:**
    
    - Raise the awareness of all employees about the cost of defects. Use posters, booklets, and meetings to make "Quality" the main topic of conversation.
        
- **Step 6: Corrective Action:**
    
    - Establish a systematic method to resolve the problems identified in Steps 3 and 4. Move from "band-aid" fixes to permanent solutions.
        
- **Step 7: Zero Defects Planning:**
    
    - Form an ad-hoc committee to plan a "Zero Defects Day." This is a specific event to signal a change in company culture.
        
- **Step 8: Supervisor Training:**
    
    - Train all supervisors and managers on how to implement the Zero Defects program and how to explain it to their teams.
        
- **Step 9: Zero Defects Day (ZD Day):**
    
    - Hold a company-wide event where all employees sign a pledge to commit to Zero Defects. It is a symbolic "New Beginning."
        
- **Step 10: Goal Setting:**
    
    - Ask every individual and team to set specific, measurable improvement goals for themselves (e.g., "I will reduce data entry errors by 50%").
        
- **Step 11: Error Cause Removal:**
    
    - Create a simple system for employees to tell management about the problems that prevent them from doing error-free work. Management _must_ respond to these inputs quickly (within 24 hours).
        
- **Step 12: Recognition:**
    
    - Establish award programs to recognize those who meet their goals or perform outstanding acts of quality. Do _not_ use money; use trophies, dinners, or public praise.
        
- **Step 13: Quality Councils:**
    
    - Bring together quality professionals and team leaders regularly to share problems, ideas, and best practices.
        
- **Step 14: Do It All Over Again:**
    
    - Quality improvement never ends. Once you reach Step 13, the program typically takes 12-18 months. By then, turnover and changes mean you must start back at Step 1 to keep the momentum going.
        

4. Conclusion

Crosby’s approach is behavioral. He believed that if you treat workers like adults and set the expectation of "Zero Defects," they will rise to the challenge. His mantra: "Quality is free. It is not a gift, but it is free. What costs money are the unquality things—all the actions that involve not doing jobs right the first time."
