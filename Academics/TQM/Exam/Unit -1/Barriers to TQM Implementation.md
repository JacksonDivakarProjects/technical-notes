### **Question 5: Barriers to TQM Implementation**

**Importance:**

> _"This is a critical 'Reality Check' question in Unit 1. While other questions ask 'What to do,' this question asks 'What goes wrong.' It appears frequently in Section B because it tests your practical understanding of organizational behavior. Examiners look for specific, realistic reasons why TQM fails, not just vague statements."_

#### **The Answer**

1. Introduction

Despite the proven benefits of Total Quality Management (TQM), many organizations struggle to implement it successfully. TQM is not just a set of tools; it is a cultural transformation. The failure rate of TQM implementation is often cited as high as 60-70%, primarily due to the resistance to change and lack of sustained leadership.

2. Major Barriers to TQM Implementation

To score full marks, you should detail at least 8-10 distinct barriers.

- **1. Lack of Management Commitment:**
    
    - This is the single biggest barrier. If the CEO and top managers view TQM as just "another task" to be delegated to lower staff, it will fail.
        
    - _Key Issue:_ Management talks about quality but continues to reward quantity (production volume) over quality.
        
- **2. Inability to Change Organizational Culture:**
    
    - TQM requires a shift from "hiding mistakes" to "exposing and fixing mistakes."
        
    - Many companies have a culture of fear where employees are blamed for errors. Changing this mindset takes years, and many organizations give up too soon.
        
- **3. Viewing TQM as a "Quick Fix":**
    
    - TQM is a marathon, not a sprint. Many managers expect financial results within 6 months. When they don't see immediate profits, they abandon the program.
        
    - _Reality:_ TQM typically takes 3-5 years to show significant ROI.
        
- **4. Improper Planning:**
    
    - Jumping into TQM without a clear roadmap or "Steering Committee" leads to chaos.
        
    - Companies often try to implement all TQM tools (Six Sigma, 5S, QFD) at once, overwhelming the employees.
        
- **5. Lack of Continuous Training:**
    
    - Training is often treated as a one-time event (e.g., a 2-day workshop).
        
    - TQM requires continuous education. If employees are not trained on _how_ to use the tools (like Pareto Charts or Fishbone diagrams), they cannot solve problems effectively.
        
- **6. Incompatible Organizational Structure (Silos):**
    
    - Traditional companies are organized by rigid departments (Design, Sales, Production) that don't talk to each other.
        
    - TQM requires cross-functional teamwork. If "Silos" exist, departments compete against each other instead of collaborating.
        
- **7. Ineffective Measurement Techniques:**
    
    - "You cannot manage what you cannot measure."
        
    - Many companies fail because they don't have good data. They rely on "gut feelings" or outdated reports rather than real-time statistical data (facts-based decision making).
        
- **8. Paying Inadequate Attention to Internal Customers:**
    
    - Companies focus only on the external customer (who buys the product) but ignore the internal customer (the next employee in the production line).
        
    - If Employee A passes a defective part to Employee B, the final quality suffers.
        
- **9. Inadequate Use of Empowerment and Teamwork:**
    
    - Managers are often afraid to lose power. They refuse to let shop-floor workers make decisions.
        
    - Without empowerment, workers become robots who just follow orders and stop caring about improvements.
        
- **10. Failure to Continually Improve:**
    
    - Some companies reach a certain standard (e.g., ISO 9001 certification) and then stop. They become complacent.
        
    - TQM requires _Kaizen_ (continuous improvement). Stopping means falling behind competitors.
        

3. Conclusion

The road to TQM is blocked by human and organizational habits, not technical difficulties. Success requires a patient, committed leadership that treats Quality as a long-term strategic goal rather than a short-term tactical fix.
