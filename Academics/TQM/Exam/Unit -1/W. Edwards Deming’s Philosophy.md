### **Question 2: W. Edwards Deming’s Philosophy (The 14 Points)**

**Importance:**

> _"Dr. W. Edwards Deming is considered the father of the quality evolution. In your question bank, this appears as 'Explain in detail about Deming philosophy' or 'Discuss Deming’s 14 points'. This is a pure memory-based question. If you can list all 14 points correctly with a one-line explanation for each, you get full marks. It is long, but it is the 'Constitution' of TQM."_

#### **The Answer**

1. Introduction

Dr. W. Edwards Deming was an American statistician who is credited with the rise of Japan as a manufacturing power after WWII. His philosophy is based on the idea that management is responsible for 85% of quality problems, while workers are responsible for only 15%. His philosophy is summarized in his famous "14 Points for Management."

**2. Deming’s 14 Points for Management**

- **1. Create Consistency of Purpose:**
    
    - Organizations must not just focus on short-term profits. They must have a long-term plan to stay in business, provide jobs, and improve products. Innovation requires constant dedication, not spur-of-the-moment decisions.
        
- **2. Adopt the New Philosophy:**
    
    - We are in a new economic age. Management must wake up to the challenge, learn their responsibilities, and take on leadership for change. We can no longer tolerate delays, mistakes, and defective workmanship.
        
- **3. Cease Dependence on Inspection:**
    
    - Inspection is too late; the quality is already built (or not built) into the product. Instead of inspecting bad quality _out_ at the end, build quality _in_ from the start.
        
- **4. End the Practice of Awarding Business on Price Alone:**
    
    - Do not just buy from the cheapest supplier. Instead, move toward a single supplier for any one item and build a long-term relationship of loyalty and trust. Cheap materials often lead to poor quality downstream.
        
- **5. Improve Constantly and Forever:**
    
    - Improve the system of production and service constantly to improve quality and productivity, and thus constantly decrease costs. Continuous improvement (Kaizen) is not a one-time project; it is a forever habit.
        
- **6. Institute Training on the Job:**
    
    - Workers often learn from other workers who were never trained properly. Management must provide modern methods of training to ensure everyone knows _how_ to do their job correctly.
        
- **7. Institute Leadership:**
    
    - The job of a supervisor is not to tell people what to do or to punish them, but to lead. Leadership means helping people and machines do a better job. Remove the barriers that prevent workers from taking pride in their work.
        
- **8. Drive Out Fear:**
    
    - Many employees are afraid to ask questions or report problems because they fear blame. If people are afraid, they will hide defects. Management must create an environment where everyone feels secure to speak up.
        
- **9. Break Down Barriers Between Departments:**
    
    - Departments (Sales, Design, Production) often compete with each other or work in silos. They must work as a team. Design must know what Production can actually build; Sales must know what Design can actually create.
        
- **10. Eliminate Slogans, Exhortations, and Targets:**
    
    - Posters like "Zero Defects" or "Do it Right the First Time" create adversarial relationships. They imply that workers are lazy. The real cause of low quality is usually the _system_, which is beyond the worker's control.
        
- **11. Eliminate Numerical Quotas:**
    
    - (a) For the workforce: Quotas (e.g., "Make 100 parts per hour") force workers to cut corners and ignore quality just to meet the number.
        
    - (b) For management: Eliminate "Management by Objectives" (MBO). Focus on leadership instead.
        
- **12. Remove Barriers to Pride of Workmanship:**
    
    - A worker wants to do a good job, but they are often given broken tools, bad materials, or confusing instructions. Abolish the annual merit rating system, which destroys teamwork and fosters bitter competition.
        
- **13. Institute a Vigorous Program of Education:**
    
    - This is different from "training" (Point 6). This refers to self-improvement. Encourage employees to learn new skills, go back to college, and broaden their minds.
        
- **14. Take Action to Accomplish the Transformation:**
    
    - Put everybody in the company to work to accomplish the transformation. The transformation is everybody's job. Top management must organize a critical mass of people to push the new philosophy.
        

3. Deming’s Chain Reaction

Deming proved that quality leads to jobs. You should draw this flowchart to visually summarize his philosophy:

- Improve Quality $\rightarrow$ Costs Decrease (less rework) $\rightarrow$ Productivity Improves $\rightarrow$ Capture Market with Better Quality and Lower Price $\rightarrow$ Stay in Business $\rightarrow$ Provide Jobs.
    

4. Conclusion

Deming taught that higher quality leads to lower costs, not higher costs. His 14 points shifted the focus from "blaming the worker" to "fixing the system."

### **Suggestions to Improve Marks**

1. **Grouping:** It is hard to memorize 14 random points. Group them mentally:
    
    - **Philosophy:** (1, 2, 14)
        
    - **Process:** (3, 4, 5)
        
    - **People/HR:** (6, 7, 13)
        
    - **Culture:** (8, 9, 10, 11, 12)
        
    - _Note: Do not write these groups in the exam, just use them to recall the points._
        
2. **The "Chain Reaction" Diagram:** This is your secret weapon. Most students list the points. If you draw the "Chain Reaction" at the end, it proves you understand the _result_ of the philosophy.
    
3. **Keywords:** Underline the key phrase in each point (e.g., **"Drive out fear"**, **"Constantly and forever"**). This helps the examiner scan your paper in seconds.
