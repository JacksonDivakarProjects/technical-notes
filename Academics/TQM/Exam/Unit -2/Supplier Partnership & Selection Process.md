### **Question 5: Supplier Partnership & Selection Process**

**Importance:**

> _"In the old days, companies treated suppliers as enemies ('I win, you lose'). TQM changed this to 'Partnership' ('Win-Win'). This question is crucial because roughly 60% of a product's cost comes from purchased materials. Examiners want to see the **Supplier Selection Criteria** and the **Partnering Principles**."_

#### **The Question**

**What is Supplier Partnering? Explain the principles of customer-supplier relations. Discuss the detailed process of Supplier Selection and Rating.**

---

#### **The Answer**

1. Introduction

Supplier Partnering is a long-term commitment between two organizations for the purpose of achieving specific shared goals. It replaces the traditional "adversarial" relationship (where the buyer tries to squeeze the lowest price) with a relationship based on trust, shared risks, and mutual profits.

- **Deming's Point 4:** _"End the practice of awarding business on the basis of price tag. Instead, move toward a single supplier."_
    

2. Principles of Customer-Supplier Relations (Dr. Ishikawa’s 10 Principles)

To score high, list at least 5-6 of these key principles:

1. Both the customer and supplier are fully responsible for the control of quality.
    
2. Both should be independent and respect each other’s independence.
    
3. The customer is responsible for providing clear and sufficient requirements (Specs).
    
4. Both should enter into a non-adversarial contract (Win-Win).
    
5. The supplier is responsible for providing the quality that will satisfy the customer.
    
6. Both should decide the method of evaluating quality (testing methods) beforehand.
    

7. The Supplier Selection Process

Selecting the right partner is more important than getting the lowest price.

- **Step 1: Survey Stage (Identification)**
    
    - Identify potential suppliers who have the _capability_ to make the product.
        
    - Use trade journals, internet databases, and past history.
        
- **Step 2: Enquiry Stage (Screening)**
    
    - Send a **Request for Quotation (RFQ)** or Request for Proposal (RFP).
        
    - Ask for their financial stability, technical capability, and quality history.
        
- **Step 3: Evaluation & Selection (The Audit)**
    
    - Visit the supplier's factory (Site Audit).
        
    - Check their machinery, meet their management, and review their Quality System (Are they ISO 9001 certified?).
        
    - **Criteria for Selection:**
        
        1. **Quality Capability:** (Can they meet the specs?)
            
        2. **Delivery Reliability:** (Do they deliver on time?)
            
        3. **Cost/Price:** (Is it competitive?)
            
        4. **Service/Flexibility:** (Can they handle rush orders?)
            
- **Step 4: Probation & Performance Rating**
    
    - Place a small "Trial Order."
        
    - Monitor the results closely before giving a long-term contract.
        

4. Supplier Rating (The Scorecard)

Once a supplier is selected, they must be continuously rated.

- **Weighted Point Method:** This is the standard tool.
    
    - Assign a "Weight" to each factor (e.g., Quality = 40%, Price = 30%, Delivery = 30%).
        
    - Give a score (1-10) for each.
        
    - **Formula:** $\text{Total Score} = (W_1 \times S_1) + (W_2 \times S_2) + \dots$
        
- **Example:**
    
    - **Supplier A:** Quality (9/10), Price (6/10). High Quality, Expensive.
        
    - **Supplier B:** Quality (5/10), Price (10/10). Low Quality, Cheap.
        
    - _TQM chooses Supplier A._
        

**5. Partnering Benefits**

- **Reduced Inspection:** If you trust the supplier's quality process, you don't need to inspect every incoming shipment (Just-In-Time ready).
    
- **Innovation:** Suppliers are experts in their field; they can suggest better designs to save cost.
    
- **Stability:** Long-term contracts allow the supplier to invest in better machinery.
    

6. Conclusion

In TQM, the supplier is treated as an extension of the factory. If the supplier fails, the company fails. Therefore, "Sourcing" transforms into "Supply Chain Management."
